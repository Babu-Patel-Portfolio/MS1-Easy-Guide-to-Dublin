Easy Guide to Dublin

Enough Information to get you started in this vibrant city


Get Started

Eat

Dublin offers offers a wide range of food with many restaurants offering lunchtime and early evening set menus. Have a leisurely walk around and make your choice. Here are a few of my personal favourites.

Fast Food

Supermac’s is the Irish equivalent of the American fast food restaurants, offering a combination of the burger joint, pizzeria, fried chicken restaurant, ice cream parlour and sandwich and coffee shop .

The American influenced Irish franchise, Eddie Rocket’s City Diner is worth a visit. A little more expensive than Burger King or McDonald’s or Burger King, but it’s a classier environment and the food is tasty.

Buffets

The Moore Street Mall a less than glamorous location but its foodhall is a real find. The food is inexpensive, authentic and delicious. The seating area may feel a little cramped and busy.

People tend to eat and go, rather than linger. Low cost high volume is what the owners aim for, offering Brazilian, Indian, Pakistani and Chinese cuisine.

Restaurants

Zaytoon Persian Restaurant is in the heart of touristic Temple Bar and is frequented by tourists, pub-crawlers and nightclubbers. Not the cheapest, but probably the best place to eat a freshly cooked kebab!

Medina Street Food Co. is probably the best value Indian subcontinent restaurant in Dublin, offering traditional vegetarian and Indian curries, in an informal alcohol free setting!

Drink

Dublin is famous for its nightlife and pubs. Whatever the occasion or taste, there will be a pub to suit your needs. Pubs to sit in and have a pint and a chat or traditional Irish food and entertainment. There are far too many to mention, but here are a few to give you an idea of what’s on offer.

Traditional Pubs

The Foggy Dew is a popular bar but always seems a little too loud and a bit packed. Located around Temple Bar.

Doyles is a wonderfully quaint pub, ideal for a quiet pint on a leisurely afternoon. Located on College Green.

The Long Stone is just beside Trinity College, full of students and locals. Good for a few pints, though you may struggle to find a seat.

Tourist Pubs

Fitzsimons is a good place to watch the football and have a few drinks. Live music nights. Located on Eustace St in Temple Bar.

The Oliver St. John Gogarty markets itself to tourists as a traditional Irish bar, offering Irish music, food and drink.

The Auld Dubliner offers Traditional Irish Music performances. Say no more. Located at 17 Anglesea Street, Temple Bar.

Modern Pubs

The Odeon used to be a railway station. Opens till late. Moderately priced compared to Temple Bar pubs. Located on Harcourt Street.

Dakota is a sophisticated bar located on South William Street. Great for an afternoon or evening drink.

Cafe en Seine has beautiful décor that nicely complements the beautiful crowd. Cool place to hang out. Located on Dawson Street.

Shop

Dublin does offer a lot of shopping alternatives, though goods here are expensive when compared to north of the border and the rest of the UK. Favourable exchange rate between the euro and the pound have helped and there are regular sales and special offers.

Centres

The Jervis Shopping Centre is on the northside, on Henry Street and Jervis Street and is the largest shopping centre in the city-centre with two floors of shops and one dedicated to a restaurant area.

The Ilac Shopping Centre is also on Henry Street and is the oldest shopping centre in the city-centre.

Stephens Green Shopping Centre is on the southside at the top of Grafton Street and across from Stephens Green. It is one of the newer complexes with three floors.

Streets

Grafton Street Probably the nicest place to shop in Dublin. Upmarket shops like Brown Thomas have an aspirational feel about them. You may not be able to afford the goods but it’s fun window shopping. There are plenty of swanky cafes and restaurants nearby.

O'Connell Street is the main thoroughfare in Dublin city. Famous for the Spire of Dublin and the General Post Office building, the streets off the main thoroughfare are also of interest to shoppers. Henry Street and Talbot Street contain the usual High Street shops and lead to the Jervis and Ilac shopping centres.

Travel

Dublin city is served by a number of travel options providing good transport links within the city and to the rest of the country, though not always good value. Again it's worth checking out seasonal offers, particularly when travelling by train during the summer months.

Bus

Dublin Bus is the main provider of a regular public bus service within the city. A reduced service operates at the weekend. There is also a night service known as Nightlink and operates after these hours Mon-Sat, and bank holiday Sundays.

Luas

One of the most popular forms of public transport, the Luas is a Light Rail Transit System, essentially a tram service running on separate tracks and roads. There are just two lines, red and green. Both run from the city-centre. Dart

Dart

Dublin Area Rapid Transit (DART) is an electrified train service which runs along the coast of the city from Malahide and Howth in the North, southwards to Greystones, County Wicklow.
